# Abstract

This paper examines Wicrypt, a decentralized mobile internet sharing and monetization platform conceived and developed in Enugu, Nigeria, that enables individuals and businesses to function as micro-internet service providers. Founded in 2018 by Ugochukwu Aronu and his co-founders in response to the persistent connectivity challenges facing Nigerian communities, Wicrypt leverages blockchain technology and custom hardware to allow users to share purchased internet bandwidth with others, earning income in the process. The paper analyses the contextual and historical conditions that gave rise to the technology, evaluates its key design decisions, and critically examines how it shapes user behaviour, social interactions, and power relations within Enugu State and beyond. Drawing on locally grounded evidence from app reviews, community testimony, and institutional reports from the Enugu context, the paper argues that while Wicrypt represents a significant African innovation in decentralized connectivity, its design embeds trade-offs that simultaneously empower digitally literate, economically capable users and risk excluding the very populations it claims to serve. The paper concludes by proposing alternative design choices that could produce more equitable social, ethical, and economic outcomes.

## Introduction

Africa's digital divide remains one of the most consequential barriers to social and economic progress on the continent. According to Broadband Commission for Sustainable Development (2023), approximately 870 million Africans remain unconnected to the internet, a gap driven not only by limited infrastructure but also by the prohibitive cost of data, low digital literacy, and the absence of locally relevant content. In Nigeria specifically, despite being Africa's largest economy, internet penetration remains uneven, with rural and peri-urban communities consistently underserved by traditional telecommunications operators whose profit-driven models prioritise densely populated urban centres (Aker & Mbiti, 2010; Oyelaran-Oyeyinka & Adeya, 2004). It is within this landscape of infrastructural inadequacy and market failure that Wicrypt emerged—not from a Silicon Valley incubator or a multinational research laboratory, but from Enugu, a city in southeastern Nigeria where the gap between digital aspiration and digital reality is experienced daily.

Wicrypt is a decentralized physical infrastructure network (DePIN) that allows individuals and small businesses to share their purchased internet bandwidth through dedicated hotspot devices, effectively transforming ordinary users into micro-internet service providers (Wicrypt, 2024). The platform combines custom hardware—routers such as the Lynx, Spider, and Gigabit models—with a software layer that manages authentication, usage tracking, billing, and device management. Hosts set their own pricing, and the system incentivises network expansion through the Wicrypt Network Token ($WNT), a blockchain-based cryptocurrency that rewards participants for device uptime and data throughput (TechCabal, 2023). By mid-2025, the Wicrypt network had expanded to over 1,800 hotspots across more than 35 countries, establishing partnerships in China, Europe, and East Africa, thereby demonstrating a trajectory from local solution to global infrastructure model (Wicrypt, 2025).

This paper analyses Wicrypt through the lens of Science, Technology, and Society (STS) scholarship, examining how the technology's design decisions are shaped by, and in turn reshape, the social, economic, and political realities of its users. The analysis proceeds in four parts: first, a contextual and historical account of the technology's development; second, an evaluation of its key features and the design choices that produced them; third, a critical assessment of how the technology shapes behaviour, social interactions, institutions, and power relations; and fourth, a proposal of alternative design choices that could have led to different outcomes. Throughout, locally grounded evidence from the Enugu community is incorporated to demonstrate the technology's real-world impact and to anchor the analysis in lived experience rather than abstract theorisation.

## Contextual Background and Historical Development

### The Infrastructure Gap That Produced Wicrypt

To understand Wicrypt, one must first understand the connectivity landscape from which it emerged. Nigeria's telecommunications sector underwent dramatic transformation following the deregulation of the industry in 2001, when the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC) issued Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM) licences to private operators (Ndukwe, 2005). The resulting mobile revolution brought millions of Nigerians online for the first time, yet it also entrenched a model of internet access that was fundamentally centralised, expensive, and unevenly distributed. By 2020, while Lagos and Abuja enjoyed relatively robust 4G coverage, cities such as Enugu—and particularly rural communities in Enugu State—continued to experience intermittent connectivity, slow speeds, and data costs that consumed a disproportionate share of household income (Nigerian Communications Commission, 2021).

The specific genesis of Wicrypt is instructive. In 2018, Ugochukwu Aronu, a software developer based in Enugu, experienced a power and internet outage at his office. His colleagues had active data connections on their mobile devices, but there was no mechanism to measure, bill, or securely share that bandwidth with others (Disrupt Africa, 2022). This mundane frustration—common to millions of Nigerians who routinely navigate infrastructure failures—became the catalyst for a technology that would reimagine the relationship between internet consumers and internet providers. Aronu, together with co-founders Chidozie Ogbo and Nweke Emeka (who served as CTO), incorporated Wicrypt in 2019 and began building a platform that layered a sharing and monetisation operating system on top of existing internet service providers (TechTrends Africa, 2023).

### Early Validation and Institutional Recognition

Wicrypt's early trajectory was shaped significantly by its relationship with Nigerian institutions. In 2019, the startup entered the Nigerian Communications Commission's innovation competition and exhibition, a programme designed to support digital SMEs and foster technological innovation within the telecommunications sector. Wicrypt placed second among 25 technological innovators, earning a ₦2 million (approximately $5,500) grant that provided both financial support and regulatory legitimacy (The Sun Nigeria, 2019; NCC, 2019). This recognition signalled that the Nigerian state, through its communications regulator, acknowledged the value of decentralized connectivity models as complements to—rather than competitors of—the established telecommunications framework.

More consequentially, Wicrypt secured an exclusive partnership with the Enugu State Government to deploy affordable WiFi services across the state. Under this arrangement, the state government purchased 400 Wicrypt hotspots to power the "Enugu WiFi" initiative, creating one of the largest community WiFi zones in southeastern Nigeria (RareBirdsHQ, 2023; Nigeria Investment Promotion Commission, 2022). This partnership was significant for two reasons: it demonstrated that Wicrypt's technology could operate at institutional scale rather than merely at the individual level, and it established a model whereby government procurement could subsidise last-mile connectivity for citizens who could not independently afford dedicated internet subscriptions. In Enugu, the technology thus emerged not solely as a market product but as a tool of public infrastructure, embedded in the state's broader ambitions for digital transformation.

### From Local Solution to Global Infrastructure

Wicrypt's trajectory from Enugu-based startup to global DePIN platform accelerated through strategic partnerships and blockchain ecosystem integration. In 2023, the company partnered with Onega Ventures for exclusive device distribution in China, seeking to replicate its community-driven connectivity model in the world's most populous internet market (Tekedia, 2023). In early 2024, a partnership with Kansi Solutions extended hardware deployment across European markets, positioning Wicrypt's hotspot hubs and operating system as tools for decentralized connectivity beyond the African context (Wicrypt, 2024). The company also integrated with the peaq blockchain ecosystem to deepen its decentralized infrastructure capabilities, allowing devices to be represented as unique non-fungible tokens (NFTs) on the blockchain (Binance, 2024).

This global expansion is significant because it challenges the persistent assumption in technology studies that innovation flows unidirectionally from the Global North to the Global South. Wicrypt represents what scholars of African innovation have termed "frugal innovation"—technology designed under conditions of resource constraint that subsequently proves applicable in resource-abundant contexts precisely because its efficiency and community-orientation address universal needs (Arocena & Sutz, 2003; Chataway et al., 2014). The platform's ISP-agnostic hardware, which supports SIM cards, Ethernet, and even satellite connections such as Starlink, embodies an adaptability born of necessity in an environment where no single connectivity source can be relied upon.

## Key Features and Design Decisions

### The Micro-ISP Model

Wicrypt's most consequential design decision was the creation of what might be termed a "micro-ISP" architecture. Rather than building its own network infrastructure—a capital-intensive undertaking that has deterred countless African startups—Wicrypt designed itself as an operating system that sits atop existing ISPs, enabling individual users to redistribute purchased bandwidth through dedicated hardware (Wicrypt, 2024). This design decision embedded a particular theory of change: that internet access can be democratised not by building new towers and laying new cables, but by empowering existing consumers to become providers. The Wicrypt dashboard and mobile application allow hosts to set billing rates, impose data limits, monitor usage, implement parental controls, and display advertisements to connected guests as an additional revenue stream.

This model reflects what Winner (1980) described as the politics embedded in technological artefacts. By designing a system where individual entrepreneurs rather than corporations or governments serve as the primary providers of connectivity, Wicrypt's designers made a political choice—one that favours market-based, individual-level solutions over collective, state-provisioned public infrastructure. This choice has profound implications for who benefits from the technology and who bears its risks, as explored in subsequent sections.

### Blockchain Integration and Token Economics

The second major design decision was the integration of blockchain technology and the creation of the Wicrypt Network Token ($WNT). Hosts earn $WNT based on device uptime and network usage, creating a cryptocurrency-based incentive system intended to encourage the deployment of hotspots in underserved areas (DePIN Hub, 2024). The token model introduces several additional features: users must stake $WNT to activate devices, and transactions on the platform are recorded on a distributed ledger that the company argues ensures transparency and security.

However, this design choice also introduced significant complexity and risk. The reliance on cryptocurrency means that hosts' earnings are subject to the volatility of the $WNT token, which has fluctuated substantially since its launch. The staking requirement—which has shifted from USD-based to fixed $WNT amounts—creates a financial barrier to entry that contradicts the platform's stated mission of democratising connectivity (Medium, 2023). Furthermore, the blockchain integration presupposes a level of financial and technical literacy that many potential users in Enugu's informal economy do not possess. The decision to build on blockchain, while aligned with global DePIN trends, reflects a design philosophy oriented toward technologically sophisticated early adopters rather than the digitally marginalised communities that most urgently need affordable connectivity.

### Hardware Design and ISP Agnosticism

Wicrypt's hardware portfolio—comprising the Lynx, Spider, and Gigabit router models—was designed to be ISP-agnostic, supporting multiple connectivity inputs including 3G/4G SIM cards and Ethernet cables. This design decision was a direct response to Nigeria's fragmented telecommunications landscape, where no single operator provides consistent coverage across all regions. By ensuring that its hardware could work with any ISP, Wicrypt eliminated a significant adoption barrier and positioned its devices as universal connectivity amplifiers rather than accessories tied to a particular network.

The company also pursued an open-ecosystem strategy, allowing third-party manufacturers to licence the Wicrypt operating system (Wicrypt, 2024). This decision reflects a recognition that hardware manufacturing capacity in Nigeria remains limited and that scaling the network globally would require leveraging manufacturing capabilities in regions with more developed electronics supply chains. However, the cost of proprietary Wicrypt hardware—which some users have described as prohibitively expensive—represents a tension between the platform's technical sophistication and its accessibility to the populations it claims to serve (BitCourier, 2024).

## Impact on User Behaviour, Social Interactions, and Power Relations

### Reshaping Economic Behaviour and Digital Entrepreneurship

Wicrypt's most visible impact on user behaviour is the creation of a new category of digital entrepreneur: the micro-ISP operator. In Enugu, where formal employment opportunities are limited and informal economic activity constitutes a significant portion of livelihoods, the prospect of earning passive income by sharing internet connectivity has attracted considerable interest. Small business owners in markets, campus environments, and residential estates have deployed Wicrypt hotspots as supplementary income sources, integrating internet sharing into existing commercial activities such as retail, food service, and transportation (Apple App Store Reviews, 2024).

This entrepreneurial behaviour, however, is unevenly distributed. The requirement to purchase dedicated hardware (with devices costing between $100 and $300) and to stake cryptocurrency tokens creates a financial threshold that effectively limits participation to those who already possess disposable income and some degree of digital literacy. In Enugu, where the minimum wage remains ₦70,000 per month and a significant portion of the population operates within the informal economy, the investment required to become a Wicrypt host represents a substantial financial commitment. The technology thus produces a paradox: it is designed to democratise internet access, yet its participation model reinforces existing economic hierarchies by favouring those who can afford the initial capital outlay.

### Social Interactions and Community Dynamics

At the community level, Wicrypt has altered social interactions around internet access in complex ways. In contexts where internet connectivity was previously obtained through informal sharing—neighbours lending phone hotspots, students clustering around a single data-enabled device—Wicrypt formalises and monetises these interactions. The platform transforms what were once acts of communal reciprocity into market transactions, imposing pricing, time limits, and data caps on exchanges that previously operated according to social norms of generosity and mutual aid (Toyama, 2015).

This formalisation has both positive and negative dimensions. Positively, it provides hosts with a sustainable economic incentive to maintain connectivity, ensuring that shared internet access remains available rather than depending on the goodwill and financial capacity of individual benefactors. Negatively, it risks eroding the social bonds that informal sharing practices reinforced, replacing community solidarity with transactional relationships. In Enugu's residential estates and market areas, the introduction of paid WiFi where free sharing previously existed has generated tensions between hosts seeking to monetise their investment and community members who perceive paid access as exclusionary, particularly for low-income households.

### Institutional Influence and Power Relations

Wicrypt's partnership with the Enugu State Government illustrates how the technology reshapes institutional power relations. By positioning itself as a provider of government-subsidised WiFi infrastructure, Wicrypt occupies an intermediary role between the state and its citizens—a role traditionally held by licensed telecommunications operators. This arrangement grants a private startup significant influence over a public service, raising questions about accountability, service continuity, and the terms under which citizens access a resource increasingly considered essential.

The power dynamics embedded in Wicrypt's design extend to the relationship between the company and its host operators. Although the platform is marketed as decentralized, the company retains significant control over the ecosystem: it designs the hardware specifications, controls the software updates, sets the staking requirements, and manages the tokenomics of $WNT. Host operators, despite being described as independent micro-ISPs, are in practice dependent on Wicrypt's continued operation, pricing decisions, and technical support. This creates what Zuboff (2019) terms an "asymmetry of knowledge and power"—the platform operator possesses comprehensive data about network usage, pricing patterns, and user behaviour, while individual hosts operate with limited visibility into the broader system.

The "decentralization" that Wicrypt claims is therefore partial. While the physical infrastructure is distributed across thousands of individual hosts, the knowledge infrastructure—the data, algorithms, and decision-making authority that govern the network—remains concentrated in the hands of the company. This tension between distributed hardware and centralised control is characteristic of many blockchain-based platforms and raises important questions about whether genuine decentralization of power accompanies the decentralization of physical infrastructure (De Filippi & Wright, 2018).

### Inclusion and Exclusion

Wicrypt's design produces distinct patterns of inclusion and exclusion that merit careful analysis. The platform includes those who possess the financial capital to invest in hardware, the technical literacy to navigate cryptocurrency staking and device management, and access to a reliable underlying internet connection to share. It excludes those who lack these resources—precisely the populations that experience the digital divide most acutely.

Gender dimensions of this exclusion are particularly significant. In southeastern Nigeria, women's access to technology and financial resources remains constrained by persistent gender norms, lower average incomes, and limited representation in technology-related occupations (World Wide Web Foundation, 2020). The cryptocurrency-based earning model, which requires familiarity with digital wallets, token exchanges, and blockchain concepts, presents an additional barrier that disproportionately affects women and older adults who are less likely to have been exposed to these technologies.

Furthermore, the platform's reliance on smartphone-based management means that users without smartphones—who constitute a significant portion of the population in rural Enugu State—are excluded from both hosting and, in some implementations, from accessing Wicrypt-enabled WiFi. The technology thus risks creating a two-tiered connectivity landscape: one for those sophisticated enough to participate in the blockchain-enabled sharing economy, and another for those left to depend on expensive and unreliable traditional mobile data.

## Outstanding Technology Features, Benefits, Dangers, and Effects

### Outstanding Features

Several features distinguish Wicrypt from conventional connectivity solutions and from competing DePIN platforms. The City WiFi mesh technology enables interconnected nodes to provide coverage across larger areas—streets, estates, educational institutions—supporting hundreds of concurrent users. This mesh capability transforms Wicrypt from a point-to-point sharing tool into a scalable network infrastructure that can serve entire communities. The ISP-agnostic design ensures hardware versatility across different connectivity environments, from fiber-optic connections in urban Enugu to satellite internet in rural areas. The advertising integration feature, which allows hosts to display advertisements to connected guests, creates a dual revenue stream that can subsidise the cost of access for end users.

### Benefits

The benefits of Wicrypt are substantial where conditions align with the platform's design assumptions. In zones where Wicrypt hotspots are active, the company reports reducing the cost of mobile internet connection by over 60 percent compared to retail rates from traditional operators (TechTrends Africa, 2023). For small business owners, the platform provides a mechanism to add internet provision as a service offering, attracting customers and generating supplementary income. At the institutional level, the Enugu State Government partnership demonstrates how decentralized connectivity can extend public services without the massive capital expenditure traditionally required for telecommunications infrastructure deployment.

### Dangers and Risks

The dangers embedded in Wicrypt's model are equally significant. The cryptocurrency dependency exposes hosts to market volatility; users who invested in hardware during periods of high $WNT valuation have reported significant losses when token prices declined, with some describing their devices as expensive "paperweights" (Reddit, 2024). The staking requirement creates a speculative dimension that transforms what is marketed as community infrastructure into a financial investment vehicle, blurring the boundary between connectivity provision and cryptocurrency speculation.

Customer support has also emerged as a significant pain point. Users on Reddit and app store review platforms have reported long response times—extending to days or weeks—when encountering technical difficulties or account-funding problems (Google Play Store Reviews, 2024). For users who have invested several hundred dollars in hardware, the inability to receive timely technical support represents a material financial risk. The platform's security, while bolstered by blockchain cryptography, also presents novel attack surfaces characteristic of distributed systems, including potential Sybil attacks where bad actors deploy fake nodes to drain token incentives (Resonance Security, 2024).

### Broader Societal Effects

The broader societal effect of Wicrypt is ambivalent. On one hand, it represents a genuinely African-originated innovation that challenges the hegemony of multinational telecommunications corporations and demonstrates that solutions to Africa's connectivity challenges can emerge from within the continent. Its expansion to over 35 countries challenges the narrative of African technological dependence and positions Nigerian developers as contributors to global infrastructure innovation. On the other hand, by framing internet access primarily as an entrepreneurial opportunity rather than a public good, Wicrypt reinforces neoliberal assumptions about infrastructure provision that may not serve the interests of the most vulnerable populations. The technology's long-term societal impact will depend substantially on whether its design evolves to address the barriers to participation that currently limit its inclusivity.

## Alternative Design Choices and Different Outcomes

### A Fiat-Based Payment Model

The most impactful alternative design choice would be the replacement of the cryptocurrency-based incentive system with a fiat currency payment model. If Wicrypt had designed its platform to operate entirely in naira (₦), eliminating the need for $WNT token staking and cryptocurrency wallet management, the barriers to participation would be dramatically reduced. Hosts could earn income directly in local currency without exposure to token volatility, and users could pay for connectivity using familiar payment methods such as mobile money, bank transfers, or even cash through agent networks. This alternative would sacrifice the perceived benefits of blockchain transparency and the speculative upside of token appreciation, but it would align the platform more closely with its stated mission of democratising internet access. The current cryptocurrency-centric design, while attractive to global DePIN investors and technologically sophisticated early adopters, fundamentally misaligns incentives: the people most capable of navigating cryptocurrency markets are typically those who least need affordable internet access.

### Subsidised Hardware Distribution

Rather than requiring hosts to purchase devices at market cost, Wicrypt could have partnered more extensively with government agencies, NGOs, and development organisations to subsidise or freely distribute hardware in underserved communities. The Enugu State Government partnership, which involved the state purchasing 400 hotspots, suggests that institutional procurement can effectively lower the barrier to entry. An alternative design might have embedded this subsidy model from the outset, designing hardware that was explicitly low-cost and intended for distribution through community organisations, women's cooperatives, market associations, and religious institutions—the social structures through which connectivity adoption in Enugu actually occurs. This approach would have prioritised coverage expansion in underserved areas over the profitability of individual host operators, potentially achieving greater social impact at the cost of a less attractive proposition for profit-motivated early adopters.

### Offline-First and Low-Literacy Interface Design

A third alternative would be the development of an interface designed explicitly for users with limited digital literacy. The current Wicrypt app assumes familiarity with smartphone navigation, cryptocurrency concepts, and English-language interfaces. An alternative design could have incorporated voice-guided setup in Igbo and Nigerian Pidgin English, simplified visual interfaces using icons rather than text, and USSD-based access that would allow feature phone users to connect to and pay for Wicrypt hotspots without requiring a smartphone. This "design for the margins" approach, advocated by scholars of inclusive technology (Toyama, 2015; Heeks, 2008), would have expanded the platform's reach to include populations currently excluded by its interface complexity. The trade-off would be increased development cost and potentially reduced functionality, but the social outcome—genuinely inclusive connectivity—would more authentically fulfil the platform's mission.

### Community Governance Mechanisms

Finally, Wicrypt could have incorporated community governance mechanisms that gave users collective decision-making power over pricing, coverage priorities, and service standards in their local areas. The current model concentrates governance authority within the company, with hosts serving as individual operators rather than participants in a collectively governed network. An alternative design drawing on the tradition of community-owned telecommunications cooperatives—which have operated successfully in rural contexts across Latin America and Europe (Navarro et al., 2016)—could have established neighbourhood-level governance councils that determined pricing ceilings, prioritised coverage expansion in underserved areas, and resolved disputes between hosts and users. This model would have been more complex to implement but would have more genuinely embodied the decentralization that the platform claims.

## Conclusion

Wicrypt represents a significant contribution to African technological innovation—a platform conceived in Enugu, Nigeria, that addresses a genuine and urgent infrastructure deficit through a creative combination of hardware, software, and blockchain incentive design. Its expansion to over 35 countries and its partnerships with the Enugu State Government, the Nigerian Communications Commission, and international distributors demonstrate that African-built technology solutions can achieve global relevance without depending on external validation or foreign investment models.

However, this paper has argued that the technology's design embeds choices that produce uneven social outcomes. The cryptocurrency-based incentive system, the cost of proprietary hardware, the interface complexity, and the centralisation of governance authority within the company create barriers that disproportionately exclude the populations most in need of affordable connectivity—women, rural residents, low-income households, and the digitally illiterate. The alternative design choices proposed in this paper—fiat-based payments, subsidised hardware distribution, low-literacy interfaces, and community governance—illustrate that different technical decisions could have produced different social outcomes, a recognition that lies at the heart of the claim that technology is never merely technical but always also political (Winner, 1980).

The case of Wicrypt thus offers a broader lesson about African innovation. The value of technologies built in Africa and for Africa lies not only in their technical ingenuity but in their capacity to confront the ethical question of who benefits, who is excluded, and whose interests are centred in the design process. As Wicrypt continues to evolve, the choices its designers make about inclusivity, governance, and accessibility will determine whether the platform fulfils its transformative potential or reproduces the very inequalities it was created to address.
